02

What you asked us to assess

A verdict only means something against a stated hypothesis. We do not assess “AI effectiveness” in the abstract — we assess the specific claim you co-authored, against a decision you actually own.

“Rolling Cursor out across our Platform and Product engineering groups over the last two quarters is increasing delivery throughput — without degrading production stability, and without pushing a disproportionate share of review work onto our senior engineers.”
— Hypothesis agreed with VP Engineering, kickoff call · 2026-05-19

The decision this informs

  • Whether to extend the Cursor rollout to the remaining groups (Data, Infrastructure) next quarter.
  • Whether to introduce a review / ownership control on AI-assisted changes to a small set of high-risk modules.

What would make the hypothesis true or false

  • True: shorter lead time & higher deploy frequency, with flat-or-better change-failure rate and review load distributed as before.
  • False: throughput up but paid for in instability or in concentrated senior-reviewer time.

We named these observable consequences with you before extracting a single signal. If a claimed benefit has no observable consequence, it doesn’t bear on a decision — and we’d have said so rather than measure it.

03

How we measured it — and what we deliberately did not do

Everything here is derived from process and behavioural metadata, correlated across four sources. We characterise the system and its outcomes. We do not audit your code.

+ What we read

  • Git history — commit, branch, and merge metadata across 14 repos (no file contents).
  • GitHub PR & review metadata — timing, reviewers, size, co-changed tests.
  • CircleCI — build, deploy, and rollback events.
  • Bugsnag — incident creation, severity, and resolution timestamps.
  • Declared AI-tooling config artifacts (see tiers below).

− What we did not do

  • We did not read your application source. No code left your environment.
  • We did not name, rank, or score any individual developer — by design, enforced in the output schema.
  • We did not judge code quality by reading it. Findings are behavioural likelihoods, never “confirmed by inspection”.
  • We did not measure everything we could extract — only what changes your decision (see scoping note).

Scoping note

A 2-week engagement cannot measure everything, and shouldn’t. We ranked candidate measurements by how much they’d move your decision and dropped the low-value ones even where they were trivial to extract — e.g. we did not produce per-developer commit counts (no decision rides on them, and they invite gaming). We spent the budget on the delivery outcomes, the structural signals underneath them, and the AI lens that connects the two.

Hotspots. Four modules account for ~52% of defect-linked commits in the window. billing-core and entitlements are both high-churn and rising in complexity — the combination Tornhill flags as where defect density clusters.

Surprise coupling. billing-core and entitlements co-change in 61% of commits despite no compile-time dependency between them. A change to one silently requires a change to the other. This is a hidden architectural decay, and it is where the most expensive incident in the window originated.

Knowledge concentration. billing-core’s concentration ratio of 0.78 means the top three contributors made 78% of changes. We flag this as a structural risk and recommend process changes — ownership rotation, pairing, a one-page domain doc — never personnel changes. We do not tell you who the three people are; that information does more harm than good and is out of scope by design.

09

What we can’t see, and what we’d measure next

An honest account of the boundaries of a system-level read. None of these undermines the verdict; they bound it.

  • Behaviour, not intent. We can show review latency dropped on AI PRs; we can’t tell you whether reviewers trusted the tool or were under deadline pressure. A 30-minute interview with the two module owners would resolve this — and is the cheapest next measurement.
  • Mechanical truth without context. System data is precise about what happened and silent on why. This is the exact inverse of a survey’s blind spot; we’d pair the two if you wanted higher confidence on the cultural reading.
  • AI detection under-counts. Tiers 2–3 are leaky — Cursor-inline and paste-in changes carry no marker. The true AI-assisted share is higher than the 34% we can attribute, which if anything strengthens F2 and F3.
  • A two-quarter window. Seasonal release patterns are only partly controlled. A third quarter of data would tighten the CFR attribution in F2 from ~70% toward ~85%.
  • Likelihoods, not confirmations. Every claim here is a probabilistic read of behavioural signals, never a fact confirmed by reading your source. That is the deliberate shape of this product, not a gap in it.
